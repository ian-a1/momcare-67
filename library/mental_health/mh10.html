üìñ About This Plan

A comprehensive, proactive guide to preparing for your emotional well-being after birth. Just as you create a birth plan, a postpartum mental health plan sets you up for success during the challenging fourth trimester. Research shows that women who plan ahead for postpartum support have significantly lower rates of postpartum depression and anxiety. Create this plan during pregnancy (ideally third trimester) when you have mental energy, then share it with your partner, family, and healthcare providers.

‚ö†Ô∏è Assess Your Risk Factors

You may be at higher risk for postpartum depression if you have:

  • History of depression, anxiety, or other mental health conditions
  • Family history of postpartum depression or mental illness
  • Lack of social support or strained relationships
  • Previous pregnancy loss, stillbirth, or infant loss
  • Complications during pregnancy or difficult birth experience
  • Financial stress or housing instability
  • Thyroid problems or other health conditions
  • History of trauma or abuse

If you checked ANY of these, a postpartum mental health plan is especially important for you.

  3.   4. Establish Mental Health Baseline: Complete Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale NOW (while pregnant) for baseline | Partner/family should know your normal mood so they can spot changes | Discuss with partner: "If I seem [specific behaviors], I need you to suggest I call my doctor" | Give partner permission to advocate for you if you're struggling.
  5. Create Your Warning Signs List: Write down what depression/anxiety might look like FOR YOU specifically: "If I can't stop crying for more than 2 hours" or "If I don't want to look at the baby" or "If I think everyone would be better off without me." Share this list with partner and close family.
  6. Plan for Sleep: Decide now: Will you pump so partner can do night feedings? | Set up safe sleep space in your room for baby | Discuss sleep shift system (you sleep 9pm-2am, partner 2am-7am) | Research safe co-sleeping guidelines if considering | Accept that sleep deprivation worsens mental health - prioritize rest over housework.

üåü Daily Postpartum Self-Care Checklist

  • Eat 3 meals + snacks (set phone reminders if you forget)
  • Drink 8+ glasses of water (keep bottle within reach always)
  • Shower and put on clean clothes (even if just leggings - you'll feel human)
  • Get 10 minutes of sunlight/fresh air
  • Sleep when baby sleeps at least once per day
  • Connect with one support person (text counts!)
  • Do one thing that's NOT baby-related (read, hobby, TV show)

üö® Emergency Mental Health Plan

If you or your partner notice these RED FLAGS, act immediately:

  • Thoughts of harming yourself or baby
  • Seeing or hearing things that aren't there
  • Feeling detached from reality
  • Can't stop crying or sleeping
  • Extreme fear or panic you can't control

Take these steps:

  1. Call your OB-GYN immediately (have their emergency number saved)
  2. Call Postpartum Support International: 1-800-944-4773
  3. Go to ER if thoughts of harm persist
  4. Partner: Do NOT leave her alone
  5. Call family backup to come help immediately

ü§ù Partner's Role in Mental Health Plan

  • Learn the warning signs: Review her list of red flags. Trust your gut if something seems off.
  • Take her concerns seriously: If she says "I don't feel right," believe her and call doctor.
  • Handle practical tasks: She needs to focus on recovering and feeding baby. You do everything else.
  • Encourage professional help: If symptoms last 2+ weeks, insist on calling doctor (lovingly but firmly).
  • Monitor your own mental health: Partners get postpartum depression too (10% of dads). Use this plan for yourself.

üí° Final Important Points

  • Print this plan and post it on your fridge - you won't remember details in the postpartum fog
  • Review this plan with your partner, mom, or best friend - make sure they know their roles
  • Bring this plan to your final prenatal appointments so your doctor knows your risk factors
  • Update emergency contacts in your phone with "PPD Support" labels so you can find them quickly
  • Remember: Asking for help is not failing. It's the smartest thing you can do for yourself and your baby.
